# CAUSAL NEXUS ARCHITECTURE — R10165672
## Garrard v Deneka | County Court De Novo Appeal
### Discourse Between Issues, Prejudice Flows, and the Evidentiary Objectiveness Anchor

**Produced: 2 June 2026 | Pre-mention strategic architecture**

---

## PRELIMINARY — THE STRUCTURAL PROPOSITION

This document maps three things:

**Part A** identifies the causal nexus between each contested issue, demonstrating that the improprieties in this prosecution are not isolated incidents but a connected chain where each departure from prescribed procedure causally generates or compounds the next.

**Part B** builds the argument of prejudice, fairness, and reasonableness from those causal flows — showing the court that the cumulative effect of these connected improprieties is not a collection of "technical" points but a systemic failure that renders the prosecution incapable of proving its case to the criminal standard.

**Part C** separately anchors the foundational proposition: that *evidentiary objectiveness* — the existence of an objective, verifiable record — is a precondition that must be satisfied *before* the prosecution can claim any procedural step was performed "in accordance with" the governing statute, "in conformance with" mandatory operational instruments, or "in compliance with" delegated regulatory requirements. Where that objective foundation is absent, the claim of compliance is an assertion without a verifiable basis, and the court is being invited to convict on faith rather than proof.

# PART A — THE CAUSAL NEXUS: HOW EACH IMPROPRIETY GENERATES THE NEXT

## A.1 — The Six Causal Flows

The issues in this matter do not exist in isolation. They are connected by causal flows: each impropriety either directly generates, or materially contributes to, the next. The diagram below maps those flows. The discourse between them is then traced in detail.

### Flow 1: Pre-intercept targeting → Opinion formation contamination

**Origin:** MDT driver profile pre-loaded before contact; CAD classification "SUBJECT STOP / PRIORITY 2"; dashcam activating at 09:20 — four to five minutes before the stated intercept time of ~09:25.

**Causal mechanism:** An officer who has already reviewed the accused's driver profile — including "Drink/Drug Driving Priors: Yes" (ticked on the intercept notes header) — before any interaction with the accused, does not approach the POFT with an open mind. The profile information is the lens through which the device result will be interpreted. This is not speculation; it is the established cognitive phenomenon of confirmation bias, recognised by courts as capable of contaminating opinion formation.

**What it causally generates:** The s.55E(2)(a) opinion — the gateway to the entire secondary procedure — is formed in the shadow of that prior knowledge. The opinion was not formed *from* the device indication alone; it was formed *through* the filter of pre-existing expectation. This is the direct causal nexus between the pre-intercept targeting and the contested genuineness of the opinion.

**The discourse:** The prosecution's characterisation of this intercept as a "routine check" (SOAF; Deneka stmt ¶2: "I decided to intercept the vehicle for a routine check") is contradicted by three contemporaneous records: (a) the CAD classification as "SUBJECT STOP" rather than "RDT" or "random intercept"; (b) the dashcam metadata showing the intercept decision pre-dated the stated observation time; (c) the 2-second gap between CAD dispatch and arrival, confirming the unit was already on scene. The discourse between these records and the prosecution narrative is not a marginal inconsistency — it establishes that the factual foundation of the SOAF is false on its face, and that the encounter from which the s.55E(2)(a) opinion emerged was intelligence-led, not random. That intelligence-led character is the context in which the opinion was formed.

### Flow 2: Absence of objective POFT record → Opinion unverifiable → Gateway unproven

**Origin:** The POFT device result — the physical display on the DrugWipe II Twin at the moment of readout — was captured by Deneka's BWC. That BWC footage has been redacted from 09:24:37 to 10:15:32 (51 minutes), covering the entire period during which the POFT was administered and read. No SOP exists for interpreting the device result (prosecution conceded on the record: "there's no such document"). No contemporaneous photograph of the device display was taken. No independent documentation of the test line / control line configuration exists anywhere in the prosecution brief.

**Causal mechanism:** Without the objective POFT record, the prosecution cannot prove what the device actually showed. The only evidence of a positive indication is Deneka's post-hoc opinion: "in my opinion, indicated the presence of a prescribed illicit drug" (stmt ¶6; intercept notes p.5; SOAF). That opinion, standing alone, is not proof of what the device displayed. The device result is a physical fact — lines present or absent on an immunoassay strip. The officer's interpretation of that fact is a different thing from the fact itself. Without the objective record, there is nothing against which to test whether the interpretation was correct, let alone genuine.

**What it causally generates:** Node 5 of the precondition chain — the requirement that the device "indicated the presence of a prescribed drug" — cannot be established beyond reasonable doubt. If Node 5 fails, the s.55E power was never enlivened. If the s.55E power was never enlivened, the requirement to accompany was unlawful, the detention was without statutory foundation, the oral fluid sample was not lawfully obtained, and the VIFM certificate — which proves drug presence — has no evidentiary vehicle through which to reach the court.

**The discourse:** This is the central discourse in the case. Every issue in this prosecution either feeds into or flows from the absence of the objective POFT record. The discourse runs in both directions: upstream, the pre-intercept targeting and confirmation bias feed the question of *why* the officer would confidently declare "lit up like a Christmas tree" if the device did not show what he claimed; downstream, every piece of derivative evidence — the oral fluid sample, the VIFM certificate, the s.49(4) presumption — depends on the gateway that the absent record would either confirm or destroy.

---

### Flow 3: BWC suppression + Ferry contradiction → Pattern of concealment on one fact

**Origin:** Deneka's BWC was redacted by his own unilateral authority (email 30 October 2025: "I authorised the redactions"), using a non-statutory basis ("law enforcement data security standards" — not a recognised category under CPA s.41A(1)(a)(i)–(iii)). Ferry's statement (¶5) claims his BWC was activated "at this time" (i.e., at the intercept) and captured "the interaction." Ferry's BWC metadata shows it commenced at 09:32 — approximately 7 minutes after the asserted intercept. Ferry's BWC therefore does not capture the POFT administration or the device result.

**Causal mechanism:** Two BWC sources existed that could have captured the POFT device result. One was redacted by the informant. The other, contrary to the corroborating witness's sworn statement, does not cover the relevant period. The result is that there are zero objective visual records of the POFT result available to the court. This is not coincidence; it is a pattern of non-production centred on a single fact: what the device actually showed.

**What it causally generates:** The suppression pattern creates two compounding consequences. First, it loads the s.138(3) balance: s.138(3)(d) (gravity) and s.138(3)(e) (deliberateness) both weigh heavily where the informant personally authorised the suppression of the central evidence on the contested element, and the corroborating witness made a demonstrably false claim about his BWC covering the relevant period. Second, it grounds the *Jones v Dunkel* adverse inference: the prosecution has failed to produce evidence within its control that would be expected to speak directly to the disputed issue. The inference available is that the evidence does not support the prosecution case.

**The discourse between the two BWC failures:** Ferry's statement and Deneka's redaction must be read together. If Ferry's statement is true (his BWC captured the POFT and it showed positive), then the missing segment of Ferry's BWC — the 7 minutes from intercept to his actual activation — has been suppressed. If Ferry's statement is false (his BWC did not capture the POFT at all), then the only corroboration for the POFT result is a statement containing a demonstrably false claim about what his BWC recorded. The Crown cannot have it both ways: either the footage exists and was suppressed, or the statement is false and Ferry's corroboration is worthless. The discourse between these two positions is the mechanism by which the defence compels the court to confront the absence of the objective record, regardless of which horn the Crown adopts.

---

### Flow 4: Gateway failure → Unlawful detention → Coercive accompaniment

**Origin:** If Node 5 fails (the POFT did not provide a lawful positive indication), the s.55E power was never enlivened.

**Causal mechanism:** From the moment the requirement to accompany was made (~09:25–09:37) to the conclusion of the interaction (~10:15), the accused was detained without statutory foundation. Vehicle keys were retained throughout — a measure for which no statutory authority is identified anywhere in the prosecution brief or in the RSA. The detention lasted approximately 38–50 minutes. During that detention, the accused was subjected to the s.55E oral fluid test, produced a sample, and answered investigatory questions.

**What it causally generates:** Every piece of evidence obtained during the unlawful detention is derivative of the gateway failure. The oral fluid sample (taken at 09:51), the Q.17–Q.21 admissions (asked during compelled presence without caution), and the subsequent s.57B certificates all flow from the unlawful exercise of the s.55E power. This is the cascading effect: a single failure at Node 5 renders everything downstream either inadmissible (s.138) or foundationless (no lawful vehicle for the certificate evidence).

**The discourse:** The discourse between the gateway failure and the detention is not conditional — it is automatic. Charter s.21(3) provides that a person must not be detained except on grounds, and in accordance with procedures, established by law. If the s.55D opinion lacked objective foundation, the "grounds" were not established by law. The detention was therefore arbitrary within the meaning of s.21(3). The key retention and the coercive warnings (discussed at Flow 5) are not independent issues; they are the physical manifestations of the arbitrariness that flows directly from the gateway failure.

---

### Flow 5: Coercive warning → Voluntariness → Admissions

**Origin:** Deneka departed from the prescribed refuse-to-accompany warning (intercept notes Q.13; BWC). The BWC records language including references to "when found guilty," "more court stuff," and injection of the accused's CCO vulnerability — none of which appears in the prescribed pro forma warning at Q.13. The prescribed warning is: "If you refuse to accompany me... you may be charged with this offence and if found guilty you may be fined and you will lose your licence for a minimum of 2 years."

**Causal mechanism:** The departure from the prescribed warning is not a stylistic choice. The prescribed warning is calibrated to inform the subject of the legal consequence of refusal. The additional language injected by Deneka — particularly the CCO reference — goes beyond information and into coercion: it communicates that compliance is not merely legally required but that non-compliance will compound existing legal jeopardy arising from an operative community corrections order. The subject's "consent" to accompany, provide a sample, and answer questions was obtained in the shadow of that coercion.

**What it causally generates:** If the gateway was unlawful (Node 5 failure), the requirement to accompany was itself unauthorised. Compliance with an unauthorised requirement, obtained through coercive language exceeding the prescribed warning, cannot constitute voluntary cooperation. The admissions at Q.17–Q.21 — "Have you consumed any illicit drugs recently?", "When did you last take illicit drugs?", "What drug have you taken?" — were made during compelled presence secured by coercive means, without caution (the formal caution was not given until Q.28, after the POFT opinion, the s.55E requirement, and the oral fluid test were all complete). These admissions go directly to Element 9 of Charge 3 (s.49(1)(h)(ii) — presence of drug not due solely to post-driving consumption) and to drug currency. If excluded, the prosecution loses those elements.

**The discourse:** The discourse between the coercive warning and the uncautioned admissions is sequential and compounding. The coercion secured the accused's physical presence. The physical presence was then exploited to obtain admissions. The admissions were obtained before the caution that would have informed the accused of his right not to answer. The caution arrived only at Q.28 — after every useful admission had already been extracted. This is the pattern EA s.139(1) exists to prevent: investigatory questioning during compelled presence, without caution, directed to elements of the charges.

### Flow 6: Reconstruction brief → Weight degradation → Proof failure

**Origin:** The entire prosecution brief is a post-hoc reconstruction. Deneka's VP287 statement: signed 17 November 2025 (404 days post-offence). Ferry's VP287 statement: signed 17 November 2025 (404 days). Lambert continuity statement: signed 8 October 2025 (363 days). The s.41A disclosure certificate: signed 17 November 2025. No contemporaneous notes are referenced in any statement.

**Causal mechanism:** The absence of contemporaneous notes and the 12-month-plus delay in statement preparation means the prosecution's entire evidentiary base rests on recollection reconstructed more than a year after the events, potentially informed by the litigation position rather than by independent memory. The statements are not records of what was observed; they are accounts of what the witnesses believe they remember, shaped by the intervening twelve months.

**What it causally generates:** The weight that can be given to every prosecution assertion is degraded. The Lambert continuity statement — the sole evidence of chain of custody for the oral fluid sample across the 4-day, 20-hour gap between RPDAS refrigerator storage and VIFM receipt — is a 363-day reconstruction with no contemporaneous records referenced. If the court cannot be satisfied that Lambert's recollection of the chain of custody is reliable, the link between the sample taken from the accused and the sample analysed at VIFM is broken, and the s.57B(4) certificate cannot prove what it purports to prove.

**The discourse:** The discourse between the reconstruction delay and the other causal flows is this: the reconstruction did not merely fail to produce a reliable record — it produced a record that is *internally inconsistent with the contemporaneous records that do exist*. The SOAF says the intercept occurred at ~09:25; the dashcam shows the pursuit was underway at 09:20. Ferry's statement says his BWC was activated at the intercept; the BWC metadata shows 09:32. These are not lapses of imprecise memory; they are assertions contradicted by objective records. The reconstruction has not merely degraded the evidence — it has generated false evidence.

## A.2 — The Convergence: All Six Flows Meet at One Point

Every causal flow converges on a single fact: **what the POFT device objectively displayed at the moment of readout.**

The pre-intercept targeting feeds the question of whether the officer's opinion was genuinely formed from the device rather than from prior knowledge. The BWC suppression and Ferry contradiction remove the only objective records that could answer the question. The gateway failure cascades into unlawful detention, coercive accompaniment, and foundationless certificates. The reconstruction brief fails to supply what the objective records would have provided.

The discourse between these issues is not a scatter of unrelated defects. It is a single evidentiary architecture with one missing keystone: the objective POFT record. Remove that keystone and the entire structure collapses.

# PART B — THE ARGUMENT: PREJUDICE, FAIRNESS, AND REASONABLENESS

## B.1 — Prejudice

Prejudice is not theoretical in this case. It is concrete and identified.

The accused has been deprived of the ability to test the prosecution's central factual assertion — that the POFT device indicated the presence of a prescribed drug — by the prosecution's own conduct. The informant personally authorised the redaction of the BWC footage that would have shown the device result. The corroborating witness made a demonstrably false claim about his BWC covering the relevant period. The prosecution conceded on the record that no SOP exists, eliminating the only standard against which the officer's interpretation could be verified. The LEAP narrative — which would have shown the MDT profile and driver history accessed before the intercept — was refused.

The prejudice is not the absence of evidence generally. It is the targeted absence of the specific evidence that goes to the specific contested element. The accused cannot cross-examine a BWC frame that has been redacted. The accused cannot compare the officer's interpretation against an SOP that does not exist. The accused cannot test Ferry's corroboration against BWC footage that Ferry falsely claimed existed for the relevant period. The accused cannot assess whether the officer's prior knowledge contaminated his opinion without the LEAP narrative and MDT logs.

This is actual prejudice to the accused's ability to test the prosecution case on the element that matters.

## B.2 — Fairness

Fairness in criminal proceedings is not an abstract principle. It is a structural requirement of the system. Charter s.24(1): "A party to a civil proceeding and a person charged with a criminal offence has the right to have the charge or proceeding decided by a competent, independent and impartial court or tribunal after a fair and public hearing." The requirement of a "fair hearing" is not met where:

(a) The prosecution's central factual assertion rests on a single officer's post-hoc opinion, unsupported by any objective record;

(b) The objective record that would have verified or contradicted that opinion has been suppressed by the officer who holds the opinion;

(c) The suppression was effected without statutory authority and on a basis that is not recognised under the CPA;

(d) The only corroboration for the POFT result comes from a witness whose statement contains a demonstrably false claim about what his BWC captured;

(e) The prosecution conceded that no SOP exists, eliminating the only interpretive standard against which the officer's opinion could be tested;

(f) The accused was detained for 50 minutes, subjected to investigatory questioning without caution, and coerced into compliance with an asserted statutory requirement that — if the gateway fails — had no lawful foundation.

A hearing conducted on this evidentiary foundation is not a fair hearing within the meaning of Charter s.24(1). The accused is not in a position to meaningfully test the prosecution case because the prosecution has deprived him of the material he needs to do so.

This submission does not ask the court to find that the law is unfair. It asks the court to find that *this prosecution* has not given the accused a fair opportunity to meet the case against him, because the prosecution has suppressed or failed to produce the evidence that would have allowed meaningful testing of the contested element.

## B.3 — Reasonableness

The reasonableness question is directed to the court: can the court be satisfied, to the criminal standard, on the evidence actually before it?

The prosecution asks the court to find, beyond reasonable doubt, that the POFT device indicated the presence of a prescribed drug. The only evidence in support of that finding is the officer's opinion. That opinion has not been tested against any objective record (because the objective record has been suppressed). It has not been verified against any interpretive standard (because no SOP exists). It has not been independently corroborated (because the only corroborating witness's statement contains a demonstrably false claim about what his BWC captured).

A reasonable tribunal of fact, properly instructed, would not be satisfied beyond reasonable doubt on that evidence. The gap between what the prosecution asks the court to find and what the evidence actually establishes is not a narrow one that can be bridged by inference or common sense. It is a structural gap: the prosecution has not tendered any evidence of the objective device result and cannot do so because the evidence has been suppressed.

An acquittal on this ground does not signal that the law lacks force. It signals that this prosecution did not meet the standard the law itself demands.

# PART C — THE SEPARATE ANCHOR: ABSENCE OF EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIVENESS

## C.1 — The Foundational Proposition

Before the prosecution can claim that any procedural step was performed "in accordance with" the governing statute, or "in conformance with" the mandatory requirements of operational delegation instruments, or "in compliance with" prescribed regulatory procedures, there must exist an objective, verifiable evidentiary record that the step was in fact performed and that the outcome it produced was what the prosecution claims it was.

This is not a gloss on the statute. It is the minimum requirement of proof in a criminal prosecution. The prosecution bears the burden of proving each element beyond reasonable doubt. Where an element requires proof that a device produced a particular result, the prosecution must prove what result the device produced — not merely that the operator believes it produced that result. The device result is an objective physical fact. The operator's belief about that fact is a separate thing. Proof of the belief is not proof of the fact.

## C.2 — Application to the Statutory Architecture

### C.2.1 — RSA s.55D(1): "indicated the presence of a prescribed illicit drug"

The word "indicated" in s.55D(1) refers to what the device did — not what the officer believed the device did. Indication is a function of the device. It is an objective event: lines appear or do not appear on the immunoassay strip, in a configuration that the manufacturer's instructions specify as positive, negative, or invalid. The officer forms an opinion *based on* that indication. But the opinion and the indication are different things. The statute requires the indication first, the opinion second.

**The evidentiary objectiveness requirement:** Before the prosecution can claim the POFT was administered "in accordance with" s.55D(1), there must exist objective evidence of what the device indicated. In this case:

- The BWC footage (Deneka's) is the contemporaneous objective record of the device display. It has been redacted from 09:24:37 to 10:15:32 by the informant's self-authorised, non-statutory redaction.
- No SOP governing the interpretation of the device exists (prosecution concession, 6 January 2026).
- No contemporaneous photograph or independent record of the device display exists.
- Ferry's BWC — which his statement claims captured the POFT administration — in fact commences at 09:32, approximately 7 minutes after the asserted intercept, and does not capture the POFT.

**Consequence:** The objective evidentiary foundation for the claim that the device "indicated" the presence of a prescribed drug does not exist in the prosecution brief. The only evidence offered is the officer's subjective opinion. The claim that the POFT was administered "in accordance with" s.55D(1) is therefore an unverifiable assertion. The court is being asked to accept the opinion as a proxy for the indication — but the statute requires the indication as the foundation for the opinion, not the opinion as a substitute for the indication.

### C.2.2 — RSA s.55E: "in accordance with section 55E"

Charge 3 (s.49(1)(h)) requires the sample to have been "provided in accordance with section 55E." The phrase "in accordance with" imposes a compliance obligation. The sample is only admissible through s.49(1)(h) if the entire s.55E procedure was lawfully conducted. "In accordance with" is not "purportedly under" or "with reference to" — it requires actual compliance with the section's requirements.

**The evidentiary objectiveness requirement:** Before "in accordance with section 55E" can be established, the prosecution must prove each precondition in the s.55E chain. The first precondition is that the s.55E power was lawfully enlivened — which requires proof that the s.55D opinion was validly formed — which requires proof of what the device indicated. Without objective proof of the device indication, the first link in the "in accordance with" chain is unverified. The claim of compliance with s.55E rests on an unproven foundation.

### C.2.3 — Road Safety (General) Regulations 2019: Prescribed form and compliance

Regulation 23 (certificate under s.57B(3)) prescribes the particulars that must appear in the authorised officer's certificate, including "a statement as to whether the requirements of these Regulations for the taking and testing of oral fluid samples have been complied with." This is a self-certification of regulatory compliance. But that self-certification is only as reliable as the evidence supporting it.

**The evidentiary objectiveness requirement:** Deneka's statement asserts: "All regulations contained in the Road Safety (General) Regulations 2019, with respect to oral fluid tests were complied with" (email statement ¶10). This is a formulaic assertion — the same words appear on every oral fluid intercept form across every matter statewide. It is not evidence of compliance; it is a claim of compliance. The objective evidence that would verify or contradict this claim — the BWC footage of the testing procedure, the SOP governing testing protocol, the device result itself — has either been suppressed, conceded as non-existent, or not produced. The self-certification hangs in an evidentiary vacuum.

### C.2.4 — Operational delegation instruments: Authorised officer authority

The officer's authority card (Exhibit 5) authorises Deneka for the "PROVISION" of a sample under s.55E(6). The VP287 statement (¶8) uses "TAKING." These are not synonyms within the statutory architecture. RSA s.55E prescribes a process by which the accused *provides* a sample, not a process by which the officer *takes* one. The authority card and the statement do not describe the same power.

**The evidentiary objectiveness requirement:** The delegation instrument — the authority card — authorises a specific act ("provision"). The statement describes a different act ("taking"). Without objective evidence of what actually occurred at the point of sample collection (BWC redacted), the court cannot determine which word accurately describes the procedure that was followed. The wording variance is not trivial: it goes to whether the procedure was performed "in conformance with" the delegation instrument that authorised it. If it was a "taking" rather than a "provision," the procedure departed from the authority under which it was purportedly conducted.

## C.3 — The Anchor as a Principle

The principle underlying all four applications is the same:

**A prosecution claim that a mandatory procedural step was performed "in accordance with," "in conformance with," or "in compliance with" a statutory, regulatory, or operational requirement is not self-proving. It requires objective, verifiable evidence that the step was performed and that the outcome it produced was what the prosecution claims. Where the prosecution cannot produce that objective evidence — because it has been suppressed, was never created, or does not exist — the claim of compliance is an assertion without foundation, and the court cannot treat it as proof.**

This is not a higher standard than the law demands. It is the standard the law demands. Proof beyond reasonable doubt requires proof — not assertion, not self-certification, not post-hoc opinion unsupported by any contemporaneous objective record.

## C.4 — The Separate Operation of This Anchor

This anchor operates independently of the causal nexus flows in Part A and the prejudice/fairness/reasonableness argument in Part B. Even if the court were to find:

- that the pre-intercept targeting does not establish confirmation bias,
- that the BWC redaction was not deliberate suppression,
- that Ferry's statement was an honest mistake rather than a false claim,
- that the coercive warning was a departure from form rather than from substance,

the evidentiary objectiveness problem would *still* defeat the prosecution case. Because even on the most charitable reading of every other issue, the prosecution has still not tendered any objective evidence of what the device displayed. The officer's opinion is still not the device result. The SOP still does not exist. The claim of compliance with s.55D(1) is still unverified.

The absence of evidentiary objectiveness is a standalone ground for acquittal. It does not depend on the court accepting any submission about impropriety, suppression, or unfairness. It depends only on the court recognising that opinion is not proof of the fact about which the opinion is held, and that a device indication is a physical fact that must be proved by evidence of what the device showed — not by evidence of what the officer believed the device showed.

## DEPLOYMENT NOTE

The three parts of this document serve different functions and deploy at different points:

**Part A (Causal Nexus)** is the analytical framework for cross-examination planning and closing submissions. It maps the sequence in which defects should be exposed and the connections that should be drawn for the court.

**Part B (Prejudice, Fairness, Reasonableness)** is the closing submission architecture. It translates the causal nexus into the language the court needs to hear to acquit on procedural and evidentiary grounds without feeling it is making a policy statement about road safety or drug driving.

**Part C (Evidentiary Objectiveness Anchor)** is the standalone safety net. If every other argument fails — if the court accepts the targeting was coincidental, the suppression was innocent, the coercion was incidental — this argument still stands because it rests on the simplest proposition in evidence law: the prosecution must prove what it asserts, not merely assert what it has not proved.

The sequential remedy architecture remains: (1) no-case submission; (2) s.138 exclusion; (3) *Jones v Dunkel* adverse inference; (4) weight submission. Parts A, B, and C feed into each remedy at the appropriate point. Part C, uniquely, supports every remedy independently.
